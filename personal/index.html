Namaste     Hello    Bonjour!

Currently a postdoctoral researcher at CNRS, France, I specialize in molecular simulation with a focus on understanding macroscopic behavior from a molecular perspective. I use Monte Carlo and Molecular Dynamics techniques to simulate multi-length and time molecular events in a system. Molecular events and interactions help me understand and predict the system's behavior at the macroscopic level. For example, we know water exists as ice by knowing how close molecules are packed together.

Previously, I used silica as a model system to establish the role of functionality, concentration, and solvent reactivity in governing silica morphology. Algorithms were developed to accommodate reactions during the simulations so that polymerization could be studied. I also developed force fields for the coarse-grained silica bead model, observing nano-particle self-assembly and percolating structure due to the physical association between beads.

Currently, I am working on a project focused on CO2 to methanol conversion-"Artificial Photosynthesis", which involves forcefield development and DFT calculations of MOF polymer composites. My expertise in molecular simulation techniques allows me to approach this project with a unique perspective, and I am excited to contribute to the field through my research.

My Research

Reaction Algorithm

Scientists have discovered some very interesting materials, such as zeolite and aerogels, by combining silica molecules. However, because we don't fully understand how the silica system works, we have to do trial-and-error and adjust different factors until we get the desired result. To help us better understand how silica molecules behave, we used the Monte Carlo technique to simulate their behavior. Our results matched what we observed in real-life experiments very closely.

By studying these simulations, we were able to identify four different stages of the silica polymerization process based on the molecular reactions and shape of the silica aggregate.

Porosity Development

The process of silica polymerization is commonly used to create porous catalysts, but the way these pores are formed is not well understood in the absence of structure-directing agents or templates.

To shed light on this process, we employed molecular simulation to investigate how pores develop. Our algorithm allowed us to estimate the porosity of irregular, non-periodic particles, and we observed that there are two different pathways for pore formation in the silica system. The concentration of the precursor and the type of solvent used were found to be critical factors in controlling these pathways.

By creating a concentration-time porosity phase diagram, we can now predict the porosity of a material based on the concentration and reaction time, enabling us to develop materials with the desired porosity.

Reaction Mechanisum

When we arrange rings in a certain way, we can create really cool materials with special properties. But the way the rings come together can be hard to predict. To understand this process better, we used our algorithm to simulate how the rings behave when they're forming and arranged. We figured out how likely each ring is to stay together before breaking apart and rearranging. This helped us make a model explaining the different steps in creating the final ring arrangement.

Knowing how to control the concentration and time of the assembly process helps us design materials with specific ring arrangements, which is incredible!

Coarse-Grained Model

Silica nanoparticles are commonly employed for delivering drugs, but simulating their formation is a tedious process due to the large system size. To investigate how silica species interact at a molecular level, we created a coarse model that treats the whole silica as a single unit. We fine-tuned the interaction potential of the coarse particles to align with the actual silica species through an iterative process. With this forcefield, we analyzed the formation of sizable nanoparticles and percolating structures.

The coarse-grain model enables us to simulate a vast system within a reasonable computational timeframe.

Artificial Photosynthesis

Artificial photosynthesis is an emerging technology that seeks to mimic the natural process of photosynthesis, using renewable energy sources to convert carbon dioxide into valuable chemicals. As part of this field, my project involves developing an advanced material that can capture CO2 and convert it into methanol under solar radiation. To achieve this, we need to gain a deep understanding of MOF-polymer (PHI) composite.

We developed an atomistically detailed model for the composite using DFT calculation and MD simulation with our developed forcefields. We explore the stability of the composite, molecular interaction of gas, interfacial behavior, charge transfer properties, and interfacial porosity.

This project has the potential to make significant contributions to the field of renewable energy and carbon capture, and our work in developing an advanced material could have important implications for mitigating the effects of climate change.

Machine Learning Potentials

This study employs interatomic potentials in conducting large-scale atomistic computer simulations of materials to enable the prediction of energy and Newtonian forces. The interatomic potential utilized is developed using machine-learning techniques and a reference database generated through quantum-mechanical calculations. Specifically, this machine-learning potential is derived from high-dimensional mathematical regression to interpolate between reference energies while also incorporating a physical base to enhance transferability.
